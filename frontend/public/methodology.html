How we model political influence

This project treats political influence as a source-backed network of money, gifts, hospitality, interests, lobbying access, party-channelled support, and policy behaviour.

The goal is public transparency without overclaiming. We show what the public record supports, distinguish direct evidence from inferred exposure, and keep missing or legally aggregated data visible.

What this can show

Who disclosed money, gifts, hospitality, interests, campaign support, or access, and which public source backs the record.

What it cannot prove by itself

A disclosure record does not automatically prove bribery, personal receipt, causation, or improper conduct.

How to read the arrows

Arrows mean a recorded connection or labelled estimate, not a claim that one actor controlled another.

Evidence first

Raw source documents, document metadata, parsed rows, and manual review decisions are preserved so public claims can be traced back to records.

Separate mechanisms

Donations, benefits, party support, interests, lobbying, and modelled allocations are not collapsed into a single corruption score.

Label uncertainty

Machine inference, missing values, legal aggregation, and disclosure thresholds remain visible in the interface and documentation.

How to Read the Network

The public app uses ordinary language first. In formal network theory, people and organisations are nodes, and relationships between them are edges. In the interface we usually call them actors and connections, then reserve technical terms for tooltips, data exports, and methodology notes.

Actor Network term: node A person, company, union, party branch, associated entity, donor, lobbyist, candidate, electorate, Senate ticket, or policy topic.
Connection Network term: edge A source-backed record or relationship such as a donation, gift, office role, lobbying registration, party support link, or reviewed entity match.
Pathway Network term: path A chain of connections, such as source entity to party branch to electorate campaign. Pathways are labelled by evidence strength.

Every connection should answer five questions: what happened, who or what it connects, which public source supports it, how strong the evidence is, and what claim limits apply.

Influence Mechanisms

Political influence is not a single event. It can operate through direct transfers, access, social proximity, campaign infrastructure, party networks, entity networks, and policy agendas. The database keeps these mechanisms typed so users can see what kind of relationship the evidence actually supports.

  • Direct material transfer Donations, gifts, debts, loans, benefits, hospitality, travel, memberships, tickets, or subscriptions disclosed at a person, candidate, group, party, or entity level.
  • Party-channelled support Money or benefits that move through parties, branches, campaign committees, associated entities, foundations, unions, or business groups before becoming relevant to representatives.
  • Access and social proximity Private travel, dinners, events, lounges, sporting tickets, and repeated hospitality can create access or agenda-setting opportunities even when no direct donation is disclosed.
  • Policy behaviour context Votes, divisions, speeches, questions, committee roles, portfolios, and reviewed policy-topic links help users compare influence inputs with public political behaviour without asserting causation.

Evidence Tiers

The app should help people distinguish strong source-backed statements from useful but limited analytical estimates.

Direct

Public record names a person, candidate, Senate group, party, entity, source, or recipient.

Reviewed

Human review accepts an entity match, classification, or network connection with source support.

Context

Record is connected to a seat, party, ticket, branch, campaign, sector, or period.

Modelled

Allocation is transparent and useful but labelled as an estimate, not disclosed fact.

Pathway Logic

The pathway model makes indirect connections visible while preserving evidence boundaries. For example, Commonwealth Bank to ALP to an MP can be shown as a party-mediated exposure pathway only when the records support each connection.

Party Money and Indirect Pathways

Australian disclosure data often records money at party, branch, associated-entity, candidate, Senate-group, or campaign-committee levels rather than as a clean payment to one representative. The app keeps those levels separate so users can see useful context without mistaking party resources for personal income.

Disclosed direct Shown as a direct record The public record names the representative, candidate, Senate group, party, or entity as the recipient or source. This is the strongest person-or-campaign-level evidence.
Party mediated Shown as campaign support or party pathway Money reported by a party or branch can be connected to a seat, campaign, ticket, or representative only when the source record or a conservative matching rule supports that context.
Modelled Shown as an estimate If aggregate party resources are allocated analytically, the result is labelled as inferred exposure. It is never merged into direct receipts or presented as money received by the MP or senator.
Missingness Shown as a data condition Nil returns, missing dollar values, and legally aggregated reporting are treated as disclosure-system facts. They are not treated as evidence that nothing happened or that hidden values were high.

Data Families

Each family operationalizes a different mechanism and should appear with its own labels, caveats, and source links in the app.

Money Donations, receipts, debts, loans AEC and electoral commission records with amounts, source/recipient entities, dates, and disclosure-system context.
Campaign support Expenditure incurred, party-channelled campaign activity Campaign expenditure is an amount spent or incurred for electoral activity. It is useful context, but it must not be read as money personally received by an MP, councillor, or candidate.
Benefits Gifts, flights, tickets, meals, hospitality Registers of interests and benefit disclosures, including missing-value flags where values are not disclosed.
Interests Roles, memberships, shares, property, liabilities Context for organisational and economic embeddedness, not proof of improper action.
Behaviour Votes, topics, committees, portfolios Policy context used for comparison and review, not automatic causal attribution.

AEC Register Pathways and Disambiguation

The AEC Register of Entities is the official public registry of registered political parties, associated entities, significant third parties, and third parties. It is the source-backed origin from which a deterministic resolver produces reviewed party/entity relationship rows without ad-hoc human review per row.

  • Exact-name match first. The resolver matches an AssociatedParties segment to a canonical party only when the normalized segment exactly equals an existing party.name or party.short_name. No fuzzy similarity, ever.
  • Documented branch alias rules. A short list of state and territory branch wordings (ALP/Liberal/Greens/Nationals branches) rewrites to a canonical parent party name and re-matches by exact normalized name.
  • Parenthetical short-form alias. A segment that embeds an explicit short_name in parentheses such as Australian Labor Party (ALP) resolves to the party whose short_name matches the parenthetical content exactly. No similarity matching - the short form must already exist in the local party.short_name column.
  • Source-jurisdiction disambiguation. When more than one row matches and they only differ by jurisdiction, the resolver prefers the row whose jurisdiction_id equals the source document's own jurisdiction. The AEC Register is by definition a federal source, so its loader passes the local Commonwealth jurisdiction id. The check is exact integer equality on a stable, source-attributed attribute - it is not fuzzy similarity.
  • Fail closed. If no rule yields a unique canonical party, the resolver records the row as unresolved_multiple_matches, unresolved_no_match, or unresolved_individual_segment instead of guessing. These remain auditable observations rather than silent links.
  • One-shot consolidation. Where the local DB accidentally grew duplicate federal-jurisdiction rows for the same real-world party (e.g. an "ALP" short-form row plus an "Australian Labor Party" long-form row), a one-shot data-fix migration consolidates them into a single canonical row, re-pointing every FK reference and preserving the curated short_name code. State-jurisdiction rows are left alone.

Equal-Share Estimates and Denominator Asymmetry

Reviewed party-pathway receipts are exposed on every current MP's profile as an "Est. exposure" line, computed under the equal_current_representative_share allocation method. This is deliberately a rough exposure index, and the response surface it back with both numerator and denominator scope chips.

  • Numerator - all reviewed party/entity money receipts currently loaded into the database, with no time window. The summary tags this as event_period_scope = all_loaded_reviewed_party_entity_receipts.
  • Denominator - the count of distinct people whose office_term.term_end IS NULL for the same party. The summary tags this as representative_scope = current_office_term_party_membership.
  • Asymmetry - because the numerator is a long historical record while the denominator is a single cross-section, the per-representative figure is not a "money seen during service" estimate. A wave-election party that recently doubled its head-count shows a smaller per-rep figure; a party that recently lost seats shows a larger one.
  • UI rule - every surface that exposes this number prefixes it with "Est. exposure" and ships the denominator chip, so the consumer always sees the size of the cross-section the estimate was divided by. The figure is never summed with direct receipts or campaign-support records.

Campaign Support: Source-Backed Context, Not Personal Receipts

The full per-tier campaign-support attribution policy lives in docs/campaign_support_attribution.md. The headline rule: disclosed direct person-or-group records, source-backed candidate or group campaign records, source-backed campaign returns, source-backed media-ad activity, source-backed third-party campaign expenditure, party-aggregate public funding, and modelled allocations are each shown under their own labels with their own caveats. None of them are summed into a single "money received" headline.

Election public funding paid to a party or independent candidate is shown as public campaign funding paid to that recipient, not as a donation, gift, or private benefit received by an individual MP, senator, or candidate. The frontend keeps every campaign-support total visually separate from direct reported totals, and the campaign_support_summary API field carries its own attribution caveat.

Postcode Coverage and Limitations

The "find your federal MP" postcode search uses the AEC's official Electorate Finder endpoint. Each postcode in the search input is mapped to one or more federal House electorates by the AEC's published service, then archived into the project's postcode_electorate_crosswalk table with the full source-document attribution chain.

Coverage scope. The crosswalk is built incrementally from a public-domain seed list and a series of staged bulk runs against the AEC endpoint, sized to respect AEC's service etiquette. As of the current published build the crosswalk contains entries for 404 distinct postcodes, mapping to 127 of the 150 federal House seats (84.7% of House-seat coverage). Each postcode entry shows the AEC source document and confidence label in the search-result UI.

Known limitations of the current crosswalk.

  • Some postcodes in the AEC's finder return no matching locality (typically PO Box / large-volume-recipient codes). The search UI labels these "no result" rather than guessing.
  • ACT residential postcodes (0200–0299) and NT residential postcodes (0800–0899) are partially covered. The project's preferred public-domain (CC0) postcode source stores postcodes as integers, so leading-zero ranges are absent from that source. Filling those territories requires a different redistribution-cleared seed source. The crosswalk currently has 6 ACT and 5 NT entries from the bootstrap seed; expanding ACT/NT is a documented next step.
  • Some postcodes span multiple federal electorates. In that case the search result labels each candidate electorate "ambiguous postcode (multi-electorate)" and records the candidates in postcode_electorate_crosswalk_unresolved for audit. The user is asked to refine to a suburb / locality where possible.
  • Inner-metro electorates (Sydney CBD, Melbourne CBD) tend to be fragmented across many small postcodes; some of those electorates may not yet have a crosswalk entry. Coverage of these is an explicit follow-up item.

The project's policy is to never invent a postcode-to- electorate mapping. If the AEC's finder doesn't have it, the search UI shows no result rather than a guess.

LLM-Assisted Extraction

For document types where deterministic parsing has known coverage gaps (multi-page PDFs with handwritten amendments, freeform contract descriptions, narrative audit reports, Hansard speech text), the project uses large language model extraction strictly under a reproducibility chain. Anything an LLM produces is tagged with extraction_method = "llm_<task>_v<n>" in its evidence row, and the project's claim-discipline rule treats LLM-extracted rows as a separate evidence tier from deterministic ones.

Where LLMs are used

  • Entity industry classification (prompt v1) — every donor / supplier / lobbyist client / associated entity that the deterministic rule classifier left tagged as unknown is re-classified into one of the project's 32 industry sectors via Claude Sonnet 4.6. ~28,000 entities reclassified at ~$100 USD total cost.
  • Contract topic tagging (prompt v1) — AusTender + GrantConnect freeform descriptions are tagged into industry sector + policy topic + procurement class. Pending production run.
  • Future stages: Hansard speech-level metadata, parliamentary committee submissions, ANAO audit findings, NACC reports, Modern Slavery Statements, Senate Estimates Q&A, Question Time. See docs/llm_strategy_full_stack.md for the full document-type matrix and per-stage cost.

Where LLMs are NOT used

Anything with a structured CSV / JSON / SDMX source stays deterministic — AEC bulk data, AusTender CSV rows themselves, TVFY voting API, ABS APIs, ABN Lookup, AEC Register of Entities. Money-flow records (donations, receipts, gifts, hospitality) are NEVER LLM-extracted — the byte-identical-totals invariant requires deterministic parsing for every row that can feed a reported total. Person, party, and electorate name resolution stays deterministic by design (the project's resolver is exact-match, never fuzzy).

Reproducibility commitment for LLM-extracted rows

  • Pinned model versions. Every cached response records the exact model id (e.g. claude-sonnet-4-6). Swapping models invalidates the cache by design.
  • Versioned prompts. Every prompt is committed to the public repo at prompts/<task>/v<N>.md. Prompt revisions ship as new versions; existing extractions stay tagged with the version they were produced under.
  • Strict schema validation. Every LLM response is validated against a JSON schema. Hallucinated fields are rejected, never auto-corrected.
  • Hash-cached responses. The full input + output envelope of every API call is persisted at data/raw/llm_extractions/<task>/<sha256>.json. A researcher can verify any extraction without an API key by checking the cached envelope.
  • Conservative on uncertainty. The prompt instructs the model to return unknown with low confidence on opaque names, rather than guessing. An honest "I don't know" beats a confidently-wrong tag every time.

The full architectural discussion lives at docs/llm_extraction_pipeline.md.

Reproduce These Numbers Yourself

Every number on this site comes from public AEC, APH, AIMS, and (optional) civic-source endpoints plus parsing and loading code in the project repository. There is no manual data entry, no private spreadsheets, no human-curated row-by-row totals.

From a clean clone of the repository, the entire federal data layer can be regenerated end-to-end with one command: 

git clone <repo-url> au-politics
cd au-politics
make bootstrap        # backend venv + frontend npm deps
make db-up            # local Postgres/PostGIS via docker-compose
make db-ready         # block until Postgres is ready
make reproduce-federal

make reproduce-federal runs scripts/reproduce_federal_from_scratch.sh, which:

  • Sanity-checks the toolchain. Python / Node / docker-compose / disk free.
  • Pins dependencies. The backend venv installs against backend/requirements.lock, never against floating versions.
  • Fetches every public source live. AEC annual + election disclosure bulk data, AEC Register of Entities (the source-backed origin of every reviewed `party_entity_link` row), AEC current federal electorate boundary shapefile, AIMS-clipped display geometry, APH House + Senate registers of interests, APH House Votes & Proceedings + Senate Journals decision-record indexes and PDFs, APH MP/Senator current contacts CSVs, AEC Electorate Finder postcode crosswalk entries, the Australian Government Register of Lobbyists snapshot, and (optional) They Vote For You divisions/votes when an API key is configured.
  • Archives raw + parsed artifacts. Every fetch writes data/raw/<source_id>/<timestamp>/metadata.json with URL, HTTP status, sha256, request/response headers, redaction policy, and source-licence caveat. Every parser writes data/processed/<dataset>/<timestamp>.jsonl(.summary.json).
  • Records a run manifest. data/audit/pipeline_runs/<run-id>.json captures pipeline name, status, timestamps, runtime, Python + package versions, platform, git commit, parameters, and per-step output paths/errors.
  • Runs the post-load QA gate. qa-serving-database fails the run if the federal House boundary count is not 150, if any current House office term lacks a loaded boundary, if parser boilerplate appears as an active influence event, if an official APH division has a parsed vote-count mismatch, or if official APH vote rows cannot match the current roster.
  • Runs the full backend pytest suite. Postgres integration tests gated behind AUPOL_RUN_POSTGRES_INTEGRATION=1, including the cross-cutting invariant that AEC-Register-derived loaders must NOT change direct-representative money totals.

For a quick local check, make reproduce-federal-smoke runs the same chain in --smoke mode (House interests PDF work limited to a small subset). It is for CI/development only, not for public data publication.

Targeted entry points: make fetch-aec-register, make load-aec-register, make fetch-postcode-crosswalk, make load-postcode-crosswalk, and make verify. Run make help at the repository root to see all of them. The full reproducibility policy is in docs/reproducibility.md (auto-update cadence, audit retention, delete-and-rebuild test, and the publication policy that distinguishes "OK for local development" from "cleared for public redistribution").

Claim Discipline

Transparency requires restraint. The system should make patterns visible without turning evidence of contact, support, or exposure into unsupported accusations.

We can show

  • Source-backed flows Who disclosed what, from whom, to whom, when, and in which source.
  • Network pathways Direct, party-mediated, reviewed, and modelled pathways with evidence tiers.
  • Patterns worth scrutiny Concentrations, repeated access, temporal overlap, missingness, and anomalies.

We must not claim without stronger evidence

  • Bribery or personal receipt Lawful disclosure is not automatically improper, and party money is not personal income.
  • Causation A donor or benefit provider did not necessarily cause a vote or policy position.
  • Hidden values Missing dollar values are missing data, not evidence that the value was high.
