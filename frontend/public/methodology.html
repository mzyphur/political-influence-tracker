How we model political influence

This project treats political influence as a source-backed network of money, gifts, hospitality, interests, lobbying access, party-channelled support, and policy behaviour.

The goal is public transparency without overclaiming. We show what the public record supports, distinguish direct evidence from inferred exposure, and keep missing or legally aggregated data visible.

Evidence first

Raw source documents, document metadata, parsed rows, and manual review decisions are preserved so public claims can be traced back to records.

Separate mechanisms

Donations, benefits, party support, interests, lobbying, and modelled allocations are not collapsed into a single corruption score.

Label uncertainty

Machine inference, missing values, legal aggregation, and disclosure thresholds remain visible in the interface and documentation.

Influence Mechanisms

Political influence is not a single event. It can operate through direct transfers, access, social proximity, campaign infrastructure, party networks, entity networks, and policy agendas. The database keeps these mechanisms typed so users can see what kind of relationship the evidence actually supports.

  • Direct material transfer Donations, gifts, debts, loans, benefits, hospitality, travel, memberships, tickets, or subscriptions disclosed at a person, candidate, group, party, or entity level.
  • Party-channelled support Money or benefits that move through parties, branches, campaign committees, associated entities, foundations, unions, or business groups before becoming relevant to representatives.
  • Access and social proximity Private travel, dinners, events, lounges, sporting tickets, and repeated hospitality can create access or agenda-setting opportunities even when no direct donation is disclosed.
  • Policy behaviour context Votes, divisions, speeches, questions, committee roles, portfolios, and reviewed policy-topic links help users compare influence inputs with public political behaviour without asserting causation.

Evidence Tiers

The app should help people distinguish strong source-backed statements from useful but limited analytical estimates.

Direct

Public record names a person, candidate, Senate group, party, entity, source, or recipient.

Reviewed

Human review accepts an entity match, classification, or network edge with source support.

Context

Record is connected to a seat, party, ticket, branch, campaign, sector, or period.

Modelled

Allocation is transparent and useful but labelled as an estimate, not disclosed fact.

Network Logic

The graph model makes indirect paths visible while preserving evidence boundaries. For example, Commonwealth Bank to ALP to an MP can be shown as a party-mediated exposure path only when the records support each edge.

Data Families

Each family operationalizes a different mechanism and should appear with its own labels, caveats, and source links in the app.

Money Donations, receipts, debts, loans, expenditure AEC and electoral commission records with amounts, source/recipient entities, dates, and disclosure-system context.
Benefits Gifts, flights, tickets, meals, hospitality Registers of interests and benefit disclosures, including missing-value flags where values are not disclosed.
Interests Roles, memberships, shares, property, liabilities Context for organisational and economic embeddedness, not proof of improper action.
Behaviour Votes, topics, committees, portfolios Policy context used for comparison and review, not automatic causal attribution.

Claim Discipline

Transparency requires restraint. The system should make patterns visible without turning evidence of contact, support, or exposure into unsupported accusations.

We can show

  • Source-backed flows Who disclosed what, from whom, to whom, when, and in which source.
  • Network paths Direct, party-mediated, reviewed, and modelled paths with evidence tiers.
  • Patterns worth scrutiny Concentrations, repeated access, temporal overlap, missingness, and anomalies.

We must not claim without stronger evidence

  • Bribery or personal receipt Lawful disclosure is not automatically improper, and party money is not personal income.
  • Causation A donor or benefit provider did not necessarily cause a vote or policy position.
  • Hidden values Missing dollar values are missing data, not evidence that the value was high.
